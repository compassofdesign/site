Your score:

The grading scale

Your skills as a designer will continue to grow as you continue to feed them with education and practice. It is not expected for you to show up knowing everything, and even then, designers who know everything can be really difficult to collaborate with. Knowing your competence level is the perfect way for you to be able to work objectively.

Though your level might not be what you expected or feel like you would like to stay at, know that you can continue to grow if you put in the work.

Your results:

Section 1: Designer History

Your level here relfects how you do with the seeking out and finding information about design. A higher score means you've probably been designing for a while and have been exposed to a lot of the crazy things out there when working as a designer. A lower score means that you have much to learn and lots of experience to gain.

Getting around people who are currently working as a designer is what we recommend. Either that is through a job, through business, through meetups or communities; having access to people who do what you do creates more opportunities to gain experience.

Section 2: Design Principles

These are the design principles, the things that are almost non-negotiable to have good design. Can good design exist without it? It's rare. Sometimes it happens by accident.

Your level here is to show how confident you are at using these skills

  • Sketching out Ideas
  • Using Line and Shape
  • Using Space to your Advantage
  • Grids and Layouts
  • Contrast and Visual Hierarchy
  • Typography
  • Colour Theory

Your action plan here is to get your theory down pat. Think about where you learned about these skills and how you can use them in your next design to move it from good to great

Section 3: Problem Solving

These skills start to get harder the more you get outside of theory and more into practice. You have to have the skills outside of moving design assets around. These skills here are exactly the kind that keeps your skills from moving forward if you don't have them down already.

  • Deconstructing or constructing a design brief
  • Basic Copywriting and writing Case Studies
  • Recognising constraints in your design process or brief
  • Understanding imposter syndrome and how to counteract it
  • Working with developers and project managers
  • Designing within a timeline
  • Breaking Design Theory Rules

Section 4: Marketing Your Skills

Marketing yourself as a designer is one of the most important things you can learn how in order to find work, gain followers, sell your designs successfully, and bring value to the design community. It helps potential clients and employers know exactly how it'll be to work with you.

Get out and start sharing what you do. There are plenty of places to start. Facebook, Tumbler, Instagram, Dribbble, Behance, and Reddit are all places you can evaluate to see if that's a decent place for you to start sharing your work. Pick 5-6 of your best pieces (or create 5-6) more and share the best of all of it.
